What is this assignment?
📋 Overview and individual work
▾

You will write a Business Plan Report by yourself. You will choose an imaginary business idea, define key business terms, and write a business plan. You will also give and receive feedback, use an AI tool in class, and reflect on both types of feedback. The whole process runs across the semester.

Individual work: This is an individual assignment. You write your own report. You will work with a classmate during the peer feedback session, but all writing must be your own.
🎯 Learning goals
▾

Writing and research: Write a structured academic report and learn how academic writing works in English. Search for good academic sources in the library database (LAB Primo). Cite sources correctly using LAB's Thesis Guidebook format.

Feedback and reflection: Give and receive peer feedback on a classmate's draft. Use an AI tool (in class, with your instructor) to get feedback on your writing. Compare peer and AI feedback critically and reflect on what you learned.

Definitions and concepts: Define 8 key business plan terms using academic sources. Write clear, source-supported definitions in your own words.

📊 What is Turnitin?
▾

Turnitin checks your report for similarity with other texts. It also includes AI writing detection. A high similarity score does not automatically mean plagiarism — correctly cited text will show as similar. Review the report and paraphrase anything that looks like it was copied without attribution.

⚠️ What is plagiarism?
▾

Plagiarism is using someone else's words or ideas without saying where they come from. This includes copying text without a citation, paraphrasing too closely, or submitting AI-generated text as your own. Always write in your own words and cite your sources. If you are not sure, cite — it is better to cite too much than too little.

PAIRR stands for Peer and AI Review + Reflection. This assignment follows a five-step process. Click each step to read the instructions.

2
Step 2 — Peer Review
In class · Write your reflection in Section 4.1
▾

This activity takes place in class. You will read a classmate's draft and give feedback. You will also receive feedback on your own draft.

What to write in Section 4.1:
Briefly describe and reflect on the peer feedback you received. Use these questions to help:
  • What were the main strengths of your text according to your peer?
  • What specific suggestions for improvement did you receive (content, structure, clarity, language)?
  • Did your peer point out problems you had not noticed yourself? Explain.
  • How did the peer feedback confirm or challenge your own view of your writing?
  • What changes did you make (or plan to make) based on peer feedback?
Length: 2–3 paragraphs, approximately 100 words each. Write as continuous text, not a bullet list.
Tips for giving good peer feedback:
  • Be specific — "the introduction is unclear" is less helpful than "the claim in paragraph 2 needs supporting evidence."
  • Comment on content and structure, not only grammar.
  • Start with what works well before suggesting improvements.
  • Check that each paragraph in Section 2 cites at least one academic source.
3
Step 3 — AI Feedback
In class · Write your reflection in Section 4.2
▾

This also takes place in class, with your instructor's guidance on privacy settings. You will use an AI tool to get feedback on your draft.

What to do in class:
  1. Follow your instructor's guidance on which AI tool to use.
  2. Paste your draft (or a section of it) into the AI tool.
  3. Use a clear feedback prompt — do not ask it to rewrite your text.
  4. Save screenshots of the AI's response. You will need them for your report.
Example prompt to use:
"Please give me feedback on this draft. Focus on clarity, coherence, and argumentation. Do not rewrite the text. List your suggestions in bullet points."
What to write in Section 4.2:
  • What kind of feedback did the AI mainly give you (e.g. language, structure, clarity)?
  • Which parts were helpful or accurate, and why?
  • Did the feedback feel general or specific to your text? Give an example.
  • Did you notice any errors or misleading suggestions from the AI? Explain.
  • How did you decide which suggestions to accept, modify, or ignore?
  • Overall, how useful was the AI feedback, and for which aspects in particular?
Screenshots: Add a few screenshots of the AI feedback. Label and number them as images (e.g. Image 2, Image 3).
4
Step 4 — Compare and Reflect
Write Section 4.3
▾

Write 2–3 paragraphs (~100 words each) comparing the peer feedback and the AI feedback you received.

Guiding questions for Section 4.3:
  • In what ways was peer feedback different from AI feedback? Consider focus and usefulness.
  • Which type helped you more with content and ideas, and why?
  • Which type helped you more with language or clarity, and why?
  • Which type helped you more with referencing and citations?
  • Did the two types of feedback ever contradict each other? How did you handle this?
  • What does this comparison tell you about the strengths and limitations of AI in academic writing?
  • In future writing tasks, how would you combine peer and AI feedback effectively?

How to structure a definition paragraph
▾
  1. Topic sentence — introduce the term and explain why it is important
  2. Definition — define the term using a credible source, cited correctly
  3. Elaboration — add examples, additional sources, or further explanation
Strong author citations (preferred)
▾

A strong author citation puts the author's name in the sentence itself. This is the preferred style — it sounds more academic. There are three patterns:

Pattern 1 — "according to"
…according to Payne et al. (2017, 467), value proposition is a strategic tool that a business can use to outline the benefits it offers…
Pattern 2 — reporting verb
…Payne et al. (2017, 467) explain that value proposition is a strategic tool that a business can use to outline the benefits it offers…
Pattern 3 — "define … as"
…Payne et al. (2017, 467) define value proposition as a strategic tool that a business can use to outline the benefits it offers…

You can also acknowledge disagreement before your chosen definition:
"While there are many definitions and some disagreement (Kaikkonen 2017, 34), according to Payne et al. (2017, 467)…"

Weak author citations (also acceptable)
▾

A weak citation places the author in brackets at the end of the sentence. Use it when the idea matters more than who said it.

Pattern 1 — passive voice
Customer segmentation can be defined as the process of dividing customers into groups with similar characteristics (Hassan & Tabasum 2018, 24).
Pattern 2 — "is" + brackets
Customer segmentation is the process of dividing customers into groups with similar characteristics (Hassan & Tabasum 2018, 24).
How to format your reference list
▾

Follow LAB's Thesis Guidebook format (chapters 7.2–7.4). Examples of correctly formatted references:

Hassan, M. M. & Tabasum, M. 2018. Customer profiling and segmentation in retail banks using data mining techniques. International journal of advanced research in computer science. Vol. 9 (4), 24–29. Retrieved on [date]. Available at DOI 10.26483/ijarcs.v9i4.6172.

Payne, A., Frow, P. & Eggert, A. 2017. The customer value proposition: evolution, development, and application in marketing. Journal of the Academy of Marketing Science, Vol 45 (4), 467–489. Retrieved on [date]. Available at DOI 10.1007/s11747-017-0523-z.
Include the retrieval date and URL or DOI for online sources. For e-books found through LAB Primo, add "LAB Primo" at the end.
